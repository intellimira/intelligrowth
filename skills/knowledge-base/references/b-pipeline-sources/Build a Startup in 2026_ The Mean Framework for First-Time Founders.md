The Build-a-Startup Framework: Your First 90 Days

Forget the one-year plan. You need a 90-day blueprint that turns your idea into something people actually want.

Days 1-30: Validate the Problem (Not Your Solution)

Most founders ask the wrong question. They ask “Would you use my product?” when they should ask “How do you solve this problem today?”

This is called the Mom Test approach. Your mom will lie to protect your feelings. The market won't.

#### What to do right now:

Talk to 20-50 people who should have the problem you want to solve. Not friends who'll be polite. Real potential customers.

#### Ask these questions instead of pitching your idea:

“Walk me through the last time you faced \[problem\].”

“What did you try? How much did it cost?”

“What frustrated you most about existing solutions?”

“If I could wave a magic wand and fix one thing about \[process\], what would it be?”

Write down exact quotes. Track how often the same pain points come up. If people's eyes light up when describing their frustration, you're onto something. If they give polite nods and change the subject, move on.

#### The validation checklist:

Conducted 20+ customer interviews

Identified the #1 pain point that came up repeatedly

Found people willing to pay to solve this problem

Confirmed existing solutions are inadequate

Documented exact words customers use to describe the problem

Validation isn't asking people if they'd buy. It's finding evidence that the problem costs them real time, money, or sanity.

Days 31-60: Design Your Minimum Viable Test

Here's where most founders mess up. They jump straight to building the full product.

Don't build anything yet.

Instead, run a Minimum Viable Test. This is how you validate demand before writing a single line of code.

Option 1: The landing page test

#### Create a one-page website that sells your future product. Include:

Clear headline stating the problem you solve

Three main features or benefits

Pricing (yes, real pricing)

Email signup or “Buy Now” button

Drive 100-200 people to this page through social media, online communities, or a small ad budget. Track how many actually click the button or submit their email.

Aim for 10-15% conversion. If you get less than 5%, your positioning is off or the demand isn't there.

Option 2: The concierge MVP

Deliver your solution manually before automating anything.

If you want to build appointment scheduling software, start by scheduling appointments yourself via email. If you want to create a meal planning app, manually create meal plans for 5 paying customers.

This approach forces you to understand every step of the process. You'll learn what customers actually value versus what you assumed they'd want.

Option 3: The Figma prototype test

Design a clickable prototype showing exactly how your product would work. Walk 10-15 potential customers through it.

Watch their reactions. Ask them to complete specific tasks. Note where they get confused or excited.

Two companies signed contracts based on Figma prototypes alone. That's validation.

#### What success looks like:

60% of people say “I want this, just tweak \[one specific thing\]”

Multiple people ask “When can I start using this?”

At least 3-5 people offer to pay upfront

You receive detailed feedback on what matters most

If you can't get this response, your idea needs iteration or you're targeting the wrong audience.

Days 61-90: Build Version 0.1 and Get Your First Customers

You've validated the problem. You've tested demand. Now you build the simplest possible version that delivers value.

#### What goes into Version 0.1:

One core feature that solves the main pain point

Basic functionality that works (doesn't have to be pretty)

Way to collect payment

Simple onboarding flow

That's it. Everything else waits.

Notion started as a better way to take notes. Stripe started with seven lines of code that processed a payment. Facebook started as a directory for one college campus.

#### Your first 10 customers:

Get them from your validation conversations. Go back to the people who said they'd pay. Offer early access at a discount in exchange for detailed feedback.

These aren't real customers yet. They're design partners. They'll tell you what's broken, what's confusing, and what actually matters.

#### Set up weekly calls with each one. Ask:

What problem were you trying to solve when you used \[feature\] today?

What almost stopped you from completing \[action\]?

If you could only keep three features, which would they be?

Iterate based on patterns, not individual requests. If 7 out of 10 people struggle with the same thing, fix it. If one person wants something wildly different, note it but don't build it yet.

#### The 90-day milestone checklist:

Validated problem with 20+ interviews

Ran at least one demand test with measurable results

Built Version 0.1 of your product

Acquired 5-10 paying design partners

Collected detailed feedback and iterated once

Identified your next five moves clearly

Most founders skip straight to day 61. That's why they build things nobody wants. You're different because you started with validation.

The Startup Validation Toolkit: Free Methods That Actually Work

You don't need a big budget to validate your startup. You need creativity and discipline.

The Zero-Dollar Validation Stack

#### Reddit and online communities:

Find subreddits or forums where your target customers hang out. Search for posts where people complain about the problem you want to solve. Message them directly asking to learn more about their experience.

#### LinkedIn outreach:

Filter by job title, send 50 connection requests with a personalized note. Once connected, ask for a 15-minute call to learn about their workflow. People love talking about their challenges.

#### Twitter/X polls and threads:

Post a specific question about the problem. “People who manage \[task\], what's the most annoying part of your day?” Watch which answers get the most engagement.

#### Google Forms + social sharing:

Create a 5-question survey. Share it everywhere your audience lives. Offer to share results as an incentive to participate.

#### Discord or Slack communities:

Join spaces where your customers gather. Spend two weeks just listening and understanding their language, frustrations, and needs.

#### The “coffee chat” approach:

Reach out cold to 50 people. Offer a $5 coffee gift card for 15 minutes of their time. Most people say yes. Record the calls (with permission) so you can review exact language later.

The Smoke Test Menu

Different validation methods work for different business models. Pick the one that matches your idea.

#### For SaaS products:

Landing page + waitlist. Show the product, list features, collect emails. Use Carrd, Webflow, or a simple HTML page. Drive traffic with $50-100 in targeted ads or organic social posts.

Success metric: 10-15% of visitors sign up.

#### For marketplaces:

Build one side manually. If you're creating a freelancer marketplace, find 10 great freelancers yourself. Then test if clients actually book them. Automate nothing at first.

Success metric: Complete 20 successful transactions manually.

#### For consumer apps:

Create an explainer video showing how it works. Post on Product Hunt, Reddit, Hacker News. Count email signups and pre-orders.

Success metric: 500+ email signups or 50+ people willing to pre-order.

#### For B2B tools:

Sell before you build. Create a pitch deck showing the problem, your solution, and pricing. Book 20 sales calls. See if anyone signs a contract for delivery in 60 days.

Success metric: 2-3 signed contracts (even if you build it manually at first).

#### For hardware or physical products:

Kickstarter or pre-order campaign. Show renders or prototypes. Set a funding goal. If you hit it, you've validated demand. If you don't, you learned for free.

Success metric: Reach 30-50% of funding goal (shows real interest).

Validation Red Flags

#### Stop immediately if you see these signals:

Everyone says “interesting idea” but nobody commits

People say they'd use it “if it was free”

Friends and family are your only supporters

You're doing all the talking in customer interviews

Six weeks in and you still don't have clear answers

The problem exists but people have acceptable workarounds

These aren't signs to give up forever. They're signs to pivot, adjust your target audience, or pick a different problem.

Business Model Basics: Lean Canvas vs. Business Model Canvas

Most startup resources mention the Business Model Canvas. Fewer mention the Lean Canvas. You need to know both and when to use each.

When to Use Lean Canvas (That's You)

Lean Canvas was designed for first-time founders who need to move fast and test assumptions.

You're pre-revenue or under $50K ARR

You're still figuring out if the problem is worth solving

You need to test 3-5 different business models quickly

Your biggest risk is building something nobody wants

#### The nine blocks:

The top 3 pain points your customers face

#### Customer Segments:

Who specifically has these problems (not “everyone”)

#### Unique Value Proposition:

Why you're different in 10 words or less

Your top 3 features (not a full product roadmap)

How customers will find you

#### Revenue Streams:

How you make money (price points, models)

#### Cost Structure:

What you'll spend money on

#### Key Metrics:

The 3-5 numbers that tell you if you're winning

#### Unfair Advantage:

Something hard to copy (team expertise, network, unique data)

Fill this out in 20 minutes. Don't overthink it. You'll update it every two weeks as you learn.

#### Download template:

Search “Lean Canvas template” and use Miro, Notion, or a simple Google Doc.

When to Use Business Model Canvas (Later)

Business Model Canvas works better once you have product-market fit and need to scale operations.

You've validated your model and have consistent revenue

You're adding channels, partnerships, or expanding to new segments

You need to align a team around operational strategy

You're preparing to raise significant funding

The BMC includes blocks like “Key Partnerships,” “Key Resources,” and “Customer Relationships” which matter more when you're scaling than when you're validating.

Most founders use both: Lean Canvas from months 0-18, Business Model Canvas from months 18+.

Your Next Five Moves: How to Stop Running in Circles

The biggest trap for first-time founders is motion without progress. You work 12-hour days, check off tasks, and somehow move backwards.

The fix: always know your next five strategic moves.

The Next-Five-Moves Framework

This comes from entrepreneur Patrick Bet-David. It's simple but most founders ignore it.

Step 1: Write down your big vision

Where do you want this company to be in 12 months? Be specific. Not “successful” or “profitable.” Real numbers and outcomes.

$10K monthly recurring revenue

500 active users with 40% retention

Product launched in 3 cities

$100K in funding closed

Step 2: Identify the five milestones between here and there

These are major accomplishments, not daily tasks. Each milestone should take 4-8 weeks to hit.

#### Example path to $10K MRR:

Validate problem with 30 customer interviews

Build and test MVP with 10 design partners

Launch publicly and acquire first 50 paying customers

Reach $3K MRR with repeatable acquisition channel

Scale to $10K MRR by optimizing funnel and pricing

Step 3: Focus weekly on only those five moves

Every Monday, review your five moves. Ask: “What's the one action this week that moves milestone #1 forward?”

Everything else is a distraction. New feature ideas? Write them down for later. Growth hack opportunities? Not until you hit milestone #2. Networking events? Only if they directly connect to your current milestone.

Step 4: Update the list when you hit a milestone

As soon as you complete move #1, it falls off. Your old move #2 becomes your new #1, and you add a new move #5.

This keeps you always looking 5 moves ahead while staying focused on what matters right now.

The Weekly Priority Filter

#### Use this every Monday morning:

What's my current milestone?

What's the biggest obstacle to hitting it?

What one action removes that obstacle?

Can I do it this week?

If the answer to #4 is no, break it into smaller pieces until you get a yes.

This filter kills “busy work” immediately. Redesigning your website doesn't remove obstacles to finding your first 10 customers. Attending three networking events doesn't remove obstacles to validating product-market fit.

Be ruthless. Your attention is your most valuable asset. Protect it.

Startup SOPs: The Systems That Scale

Standard Operating Procedures sound corporate and boring. For startups, they're the difference between chaos and consistency.

SOPs are just documented processes. Written instructions for repeated tasks. They save time, prevent errors, and make onboarding new people 10x easier.

Why First-Time Founders Need SOPs

Most founders think “I'll document things later when we're bigger.” That's backwards.

#### Create SOPs now because:

You'll do the same task 100+ times.

Publishing a blog post. Onboarding a customer. Responding to support tickets. Document it once, save hours.

You'll forget your own process.

Six months from now, you won't remember the exact steps. Your documentation will.

New team members need to ramp up fast.

Clear SOPs turn a 3-week onboarding into a 3-day onboarding.

Systems let you delegate without micromanaging.

Hand someone the SOP and they can execute without asking you 20 questions.

You spot inefficiencies faster.

Writing out your process reveals stupid steps you can eliminate.

The Startup SOP Library (What to Document First)

#### Start with these eight processes:

1. Customer onboarding SOP

From signup to first value delivery. Every step, every email, every setting they need to configure.

2. Support ticket response SOP

How to handle common questions. Template responses. Escalation criteria. Response time standards.

3. Content publishing SOP

How to go from idea to published post. Research, writing, editing, SEO, formatting, promotion.

4. Customer interview SOP

Questions to ask. How to record. Where to store notes. How to analyze patterns.

5. Product launch checklist SOP

Everything that needs to happen before you ship a feature. Testing, documentation, marketing, communication.

6. Weekly planning SOP

How you review metrics, set priorities, and plan the week. Make this bulletproof.

7. Sales or demo call SOP

Talking points. Demo flow. Pricing discussion. Objection handling. Follow-up sequence.

8. Team meeting SOP

Agenda template. Who attends. Duration. How decisions get made and documented.

Eight SOPs cover 80% of what you do repeatedly.

The Simple SOP Template

#### Use this structure for every process:

Why this process exists and what success looks like

Who's responsible for executing this (role, not name)

Frequency or triggering event

#### Tools needed:

Software, templates, accounts, access

#### Step-by-step process:

First action (with specifics)

Second action (with specifics)

Continue until complete

#### Common problems and solutions:

What goes wrong and how to fix it

Screenshots, videos, or links to past examples

Keep each SOP to 1-2 pages maximum. If it's longer, break it into multiple SOPs.

Where to Store Your SOPs

Best option: Notion

Create a “Playbook” database. Each SOP is a page. Tag by category, owner, and frequency. Searchable, sharable, easy to update.

Alternative: Google Docs

One folder, one doc per SOP. Simple but less organized as you grow.

Video SOPs with Loom (Free – $8/month)

Record yourself doing the process. Add to your written SOP. People learn faster with video + text than text alone.

The format matters less than having it documented and accessible.

The Real Startup Costs: What You'll Actually Spend

Most startup cost estimates are fantasy. They either lowball everything (bootstrap for $500!) or include ridiculous overhead (you need $50K minimum!).

Here's the truth for different business models.

The Actual Numbers for Year One

#### Digital product or SaaS ($2,000 – $8,000):

Domain and hosting: $100-$300

Design and no-code tools: $0-$500

Payment processing (Stripe): 3% of revenue

Software subscriptions: $500-$1,200

LLC formation and legal: $300-$1,000

Accounting software: $200-$400

Marketing and ads: $500-$3,000

Miscellaneous: $400-$600

You can start for under $2K if you do everything yourself. Budget $5-8K if you outsource design and pay for some ads.

#### Service-based business ($1,000 – $5,000):

Website and domain: $100-$500

Business registration: $300-$800

Insurance: $300-$1,500

Software tools: $300-$1,000

Marketing: $0-$1,500

Miscellaneous: $200-$700

Services are cheaper because you're selling time and expertise. No inventory, no product development.

#### E-commerce or physical product ($5,000 – $25,000):

Inventory or first production run: $2,000-$15,000

E-commerce platform (Shopify): $400-$800

Website and branding: $500-$2,000

Payment processing: 3% of revenue

Shipping and fulfillment: Variable

Marketing: $1,000-$5,000

Legal and registration: $500-$1,500

Miscellaneous: $600-$1,200

Physical products cost more upfront. You need inventory before you can sell anything.

The Monthly Burn Rate Reality

#### Track these numbers obsessively:

#### Fixed costs (same every month):

Software subscriptions

Hosting and domains

Accounting services

Coworking space (if applicable)

#### Variable costs (scale with usage):

Payment processing fees

Freelancer or contractor costs

Shipping (for physical products)

Total monthly burn = Fixed + Variable

Your runway = Cash in bank / Monthly burn

If you have $10K and burn $2K per month, you have 5 months of runway. That's your deadline to reach profitability or raise money.

The Funding Reality Check

#### Bootstrap (use your own money):

Pros: Full control, no dilution, forces discipline Cons: Slower growth, higher stress, limited resources

Best for: Service businesses, SaaS with low upfront costs, side projects

#### Friends and family ($10K – $100K):

Pros: Easier to raise, flexible terms, supportive investors Cons: Mixing business and relationships, unsophisticated investors

Best for: Covering first 6-12 months while you validate

#### Angel investors ($25K – $500K):

Pros: Smart money, connections, credibility Cons: Give up 10-20% equity, time-intensive process

Best for: Post-validation, pre-revenue or early revenue

#### Venture capital ($500K+):

Pros: Large capital, network effects, resources Cons: Give up 20-30%+ equity, high growth pressure, time sink

Best for: Proven model ready to scale fast

#### Don't raise money until:

You've validated the problem and solution

You have early customers or revenue

You know exactly what you'll use the money for

You're ready to scale, not still figuring things out

90% of first-time founders should bootstrap or raise friends-and-family money. VC comes later.

Legal and Admin: The Boring Stuff That Matters

You need to handle three legal things before launching. Everything else can wait.

Protects personal assets if things go wrong

Simple to set up and maintain

Flexible for taxes

Professional credibility

Cost: $100-$500 depending on state Time: 1-2 hours to file online

Skip sole proprietorship (no liability protection) and corporations (overkill for early stage).

#### Think of it as a social security number for your business. You need it to:

Open a business bank account

Hire employees or contractors later

Cost: Free from IRS website Time: 15 minutes online

3. Business bank account

Separate business and personal money. Makes accounting and taxes infinitely easier.

#### What you need:

LLC formation documents

#### Recommended banks for startups:

Mercury (online, startup-focused)

Chase (physical branches, solid features)

Local credit unions (relationship-based)

Cost: Usually free for business checking Time: 30-60 minutes

What to Handle Later (Not Now)

#### These matter but don't block you from launching:

#### Trademarks:

File when you have traction and budget ($250-$750)

Only if you have truly novel technology (most don't)

#### Detailed contracts:

Start with template agreements, upgrade to lawyers later

#### Hiring paperwork:

Cross this bridge when you actually hire someone

#### Complex tax planning:

Basic accounting software handles early-stage taxes

The goal is legal enough to operate safely, not perfect compliance from day one.

#### Content with embedded CTAs:

Write blog posts solving specific problems your audience Googles

#### Launch on product discovery platforms:

Product Hunt

Hacker News (Show HN)

Indie Hackers

Reddit (r/SideProject, r/EntrepreneurRideAlong)

One good launch post can bring 100-500 visitors and 10-30 signups if your hook is strong.

#### The coffee chat loop:

Talk to potential customers

Show them the product

Ask if they'll try it

Follow up weekly

Personal touch works when you're tiny.

The Next 90: Find One Channel That Works

You can't do everything. Pick one customer acquisition channel and go deep.

#### Content marketing (SEO):

Write 2-3 blog posts per week targeting problems your product solves

Takes 3-6 months to see traffic

Compounds over time

Best for: B2B tools, niche software, educational products

#### Community building:

Start a Discord, Slack group, or newsletter

Provide value first, sell second

Slow growth, high retention

Best for: Creator tools, niche audiences, passion-driven products

Google Ads for high-intent searches

Facebook/Instagram for awareness

Start with $10-20/day budget

Best for: Clear value prop, proven conversion, solid LTV

#### Partnerships:

Find companies serving the same audience

Offer value to their customers

Co-marketing, affiliate deals, integration partnerships

Best for: B2B, complementary products, established markets

#### Direct sales:

Email or LinkedIn outreach

Booked calls, live demos

High-touch, high-conversion

Best for: B2B with $100+/month pricing, complex products

Run one channel for 4-6 weeks. Measure cost-per-acquisition and conversion rate. If it works, double down. If it doesn't, try the next one.

Don't spread yourself thin running five channels badly.

The Metrics That Actually Matter

#### Track these three numbers weekly:

1. Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC):

Total marketing spend / New customers acquired = CAC

If you spent $500 and got 10 customers, CAC = $50

2. Lifetime Value (LTV):

Average revenue per customer x Average customer lifespan = LTV

If customers pay $30/month and stay 8 months on average, LTV = $240

3. LTV/CAC Ratio:

LTV / CAC = Health of your business

Below 1: You're losing money on every customer

1-3: You're breaking even or barely profitable

3+: You have a scalable business

Example: $240 LTV / $50 CAC = 4.8 ratio (good)

Once you hit a 3:1 ratio or better, you can start spending more on acquisition. Before that, focus on improving conversion and retention.

Your 30-Day Action Plan: Start Right Now

You've read the framework. Now execute it.

Week 1: Problem validation

Identify 3 specific problems you could solve based on your experience or expertise.

List 20 people who likely face each problem. Actual names. Find them on LinkedIn, in online communities, or through mutual connections.

Reach out to 15-20 people. Ask for 15-minute calls to learn about their challenges. Book 10+ calls.

#### Goal by end of week 1:

10 customer interviews scheduled.

Week 2: Deep customer discovery

Conduct all 10 interviews. Record them (with permission). Take detailed notes immediately after.

#### Questions to ask:

Walk me through the last time you faced \[problem\]

What did you try? How much time/money did it cost?

What frustrates you most about current solutions?

If I could fix one thing about \[process\], what would it be?

Would you pay to solve this problem? How much?

#### Goal by end of week 2:

Clear picture of the #1 pain point and evidence people would pay to solve it.

Week 3: Minimum viable test

Choose your validation method (landing page, manual concierge, Figma prototype, or pre-sales).

Build your test. Don't overthink it. Simple and fast beats perfect and slow.

Launch your test. Share in communities, message your interview participants, post on social media.

#### Goal by end of week 3:

50-100 people have seen your test and you have initial data.

Week 4: Analyze and decide

Review results. Calculate conversion rates. Read all feedback. Look for patterns.

#### Success signals:

10-15% of people took action (signed up, clicked buy, requested info)

Multiple people asked when they can start using it

Specific, detailed feedback on what they need

If validation succeeded, plan your MVP. List the one core feature you'll build first. If validation failed, review your interviews and pivot to a different angle or problem.

Set up your business basics (LLC, EIN, business bank account). Or work with a lawyer if needed.

Write down your next five moves. Be specific. Set deadlines.

#### Goal by end of week 4:

Clear decision to build, pivot, or explore a different idea, plus your roadmap for the next 90 days.

The Truth About Building a Startup

Let's end with what most guides won't tell you.

Building a startup is not a hack. It's not a four-hour workweek. It's not passive income.

It's years of uncertainty, constant problem-solving, and frequent moments where you question everything.

It's also one of the most rewarding things you'll ever do.

You'll learn more in 12 months of building than 4 years in most jobs. You'll develop skills that make you valuable forever. You'll build something that didn't exist before you started.

Most importantly, you'll prove to yourself that you can create value from nothing but an idea and effort.

The difference between founders who make it and founders who don't isn't intelligence, connections, or luck.

It's whether you validate before building, focus on what matters, and persist through the hard parts.

You have everything you need to start right now.

The question is: will you?
