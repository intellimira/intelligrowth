Every founder thinks their idea will work — until it doesn't. The difference between success and failure? Validation.

Yash Chavan asked five people to pay him for a product that didn't exist yet.

They said yes.

That's when he knew

https://www.getsaral.com/

(an influencer relationship management platform for e-commerce brands) was worth building. Today it makes $1.1M annually with nine employees.

Most founders do the opposite. They build for months, launch to crickets, then wonder what went wrong.

Here's the truth: 42% of startups fail because nobody needs what they built. Not because the product was bad — because people didn't want to pay for it.

I'm going to show you how to validate the smart way. Three weeks. Zero code. Real paying customers.

The Validation Trap Most Founders Fall Into

You think 500 email signups mean you're onto something. But only 2–5% will actually pay.

That's 10–25 customers. Can you build a business on that?

Or someone tells you “I'd totally use this!” Research shows 80% of people lie in surveys to avoid hurting your feelings.

Here's what real validation looks like: Someone paying you before the product is finished.

Everything else is noise.

Week 1: Find Money Already Flowing

Don't look for problems. Look for existing solutions people are paying for right now.

Why? Money already flowing means the problem is painful enough to solve. You just need to do it better.

Days 1–2: Read What Customers Actually Complain About

Go to G2 or Capterra. Search your category. Pick 3–5 tools with 100+ reviews.

Read only the 1-star and 2-star reviews.

Five or more people saying the same thing

“I wish it had this feature”

“I'm canceling because of this”

“Switching to another tool for this reason”

When I researched email tools, 47 reviews said Mailchimp was “too complicated for beginners.” That's your opening — a simpler tool could steal customers.

Days 3–5: Talk to People Who Already Pay

people who reviewed your competitor's product.

to respond → around

8–20 real conversations

#### Use this email:

Subject: Quick question about \[Tool Name\]

Hi \[Name\], I saw your review of \[Tool Name\] on G2. I'm researching tools in this space and would love 10 minutes of your time. Not selling anything — just trying to understand what works and what doesn't. Open to a quick call this week?

#### On the call, ask about what they've actually done, not what they might do:

“Why did you pick this tool?”

“Walk me through the last time you used it”

“What would make you cancel immediately?”

“How much do you pay? Is it worth it?”

Everyone says “nothing would make me switch.” The market is too happy. Move on.

Three or more people mention the same missing feature. You found your opening.

Rahul Vohra talked to 100+ email users before building Superhuman. Today it makes $20M annually.

Days 6–7: Force Your Idea Into One Sentence

#### Use this format:

\[Who\] needs to \[do what\] but struggles with \[obstacle\] because \[reason\]. My solution \[does this\].

“Businesses need better project management.”

“Creative agency owners managing 5–10 clients need to track revisions but struggle with messy email threads because clients won't adopt new tools. My solution creates a portal that works through email — no login required.”

Send that sentence to 10 people in your target market. Ask: “Does this describe your situation?”

Seven or more say yes? Keep going. Less than five? Pivot or quit.

Week 2: Test If People Will Pay

Interest is cheap. Payment proves everything.

Days 8–10: Build a Sales Page (With Real Pricing)

Not a “join the waitlist” page. A real sales page with pricing.

Headline: Problem plus solution in 10 words

Who it's for and what outcome they get

Three key benefits (outcomes, not features)

Real pricing

Button that says “Start Free Trial” or “Book Demo”

#### When someone clicks, show this:

“Thanks for your interest. We're in private beta and can only take 10 customers this month. We'll help you set this up manually at first in exchange for feedback. Book a 15-minute call: \[link\]”

Use Carrd ($19/year), Webflow (free), or Notion plus Super ($12/month).

Buffer tested their social media scheduler with a landing page showing $5-$50/month pricing. 100 people clicked “Start Trial” in week one. They only built the product after 20 people committed to paying.

Days 11–12: Get 100 People to Your Page

Spend $100 to test demand fast.

Use LinkedIn Sales Navigator's free trial. Find your exact customer by job title and company size. Send 50 connection requests with personal notes.

Run Facebook or Google ads. Make three versions testing different pain points. Budget: $50–100.

#### For Reddit:

Find 3–5 places your audience hangs out. Post: “I'm researching this problem. Do you deal with it?” Share your landing page in comments.

#### Good signs:

100+ visits in three days

15%+ click your call-to-action

Five or more people book calls

Less than 10% click your button. Your message is wrong or the problem isn't painful enough.

Days 13–14: The Call That Proves Everything

You've got 5–10 calls booked. Here's how to turn interest into payment.

#### Start (2 minutes):

“Thanks for your time. I'm testing an idea for \[solution\]. It's not built yet, but I can deliver the result manually for the first 10 customers. Here's what I'm thinking…”

#### Present (3 minutes):

Show mockups or sketches. Explain the outcome. State the price: “When we launch, this will be $X/month. For early customers helping us shape it, it's 50% off: $Y/month.”

#### Ask (1 minute):

“If I can deliver \[specific result\] in 30 days — manually at first — would you commit to trying this at $Y/month?”

If they hesitate: “What would need to change for this to be a clear yes?”

#### What good looks like:

70%+ say yes: Build it now

30–50% say yes: Good, refine your offer

Less than 30%: Wrong audience or weak solution

Yash Chavan got five people to pre-pay for SARAL before building anything. That validated his market and funded his first developer.

How to Actually Get Pre-Sales (3 Proven Methods)

Method 1: The Founding Customer Deal

“I'm taking 5 founding customers at 50% off for life. You get white-glove service and shape the product. But I need payment today to know you're serious.”

Method 2: The Manual Concierge

“I'll deliver the results manually for 3 months while we build the automation. $X/month, cancel anytime. Think of me as your personal assistant for this.”

Method 3: The Access Pass

“Pay 100nowforlifetimeaccessatthefoundingmemberrate(X/month). If we don't launch in 90 days, full refund. You're betting $100 that we'll build this.”

#### Why this works:

You're not asking them to buy vaporware. You're offering real value now (your time) in exchange for validation (their money).

Peter Levels (Nomad List, RemoteOK) made $10K in pre-sales before writing a single line of code. He used Method 3.

Week 3: Deliver Manually to Learn What Actually Matters

Days 15–17: Become the Product

For the 3–5 people who paid, deliver the outcome by hand.

Social media scheduler? Schedule posts manually with Buffer

Invoice tool? Make invoices in Excel, email them

Feedback collector? Copy-paste feedback into Google Sheets

Analytics dashboard? Pull data by hand, make charts

Track every minute. This tells you if the economics work.

#### What you learn:

Which features people actually use

Problems that will break your automation

The real workflow, not your ideal version

Whether the outcome is worth the price

Stefan Debois made a fun iPad quiz for his daughter's birthday. Consultants saw it and wanted it for their clients. He delivered it manually first, learning exactly what they needed. Today Pointerpro makes $287K monthly.

Days 18–19: Ask Five Questions

#### Survey your manual users:

1. “On a scale of 1–10, how disappointed would you be if this disappeared tomorrow?”
9–10: You have something real

6–8: Good, needs work

1–5: Wrong solution

2. “What's the one thing that would make this perfect?”

3. “Would you tell a colleague about this? Why or why not?”

4. “What almost stopped you from signing up?”

5. “How much would you pay if this was fully automated?”

Use Google Forms (free), Typeform ($25/month), or Tally (free).

Days 20–21: Decide

#### Build it if:

40% or more scored 9–10 on disappointment

Three or more paid customers want to continue

Your time costs less than 70% of what they paid

You can find 100 more customers like them

You're excited to work on this for years

#### Change it if:

Score is 6–8 but feedback shows you need different features

They love it but your pricing is wrong

Manual work takes too long but automation would fix it

#### Kill it if:

Less than 30% want to continue

Economics don't work even with automation

Can't find 100 more customers

You're already tired of the problem

Dropbox founder Drew Houston made a 3-minute demo video showing how the product would work before building it. Posted on Hacker News, the video grew his waitlist from 5,000 to 75,000 people overnight. That validation proved demand existed before he wrote the actual code.

Airbnb founders flew to New York to meet with dozens of hosts after Paul Graham advised them to talk directly to customers. Through these conversations, they discovered that photo quality was the key factor influencing bookings — not the feature they expected. They offered free professional photography, which drove a massive increase in bookings and helped establish Airbnb as a major player.

The Mistakes That Kill Ideas

Mistake 1: Asking Friends and Family

They'll say nice things even if your idea is terrible. Only talk to people in your target market who currently pay to solve this problem.

Mistake 2: Collecting Email Addresses

One founder got 5,000 emails for a productivity app. Launched to 87 paying customers. 1.7% conversion. Business failed.

Get payment commitments, not email addresses.

Mistake 3: Building What Nobody Asked For

During manual delivery, only build what they specifically asked for. If they never mention something, don't build it.

Mistake 4: Wrong Price

Ask: “How much does this problem cost you monthly in time or money?” Price at 10–20% of that.

Superhuman charges $30/month because power users waste $200+ monthly on slow email. Easy choice.

Mistake 5: Stopping After One No

One opinion isn't data. Talk to 20+ people minimum. If 15+ say the same thing, that's real.

Mistake 6: Asking Questions That Lie

“Would you use a tool that does X?”

Everyone says yes. It's hypothetical and costs them nothing.

#### Instead ask:

“How do you currently solve X?”

This reveals if the problem is real and how painful it is.

“Would you pay $50/month for this?”

They'll say yes to be polite.

#### Instead ask:

“How much does this problem cost you monthly?”

Then price at 10–20% of that number.

“What features do you want?”

You'll build a bloated product nobody needs.

#### Instead ask:

“Walk me through the last time you dealt with this problem.”

Watch what they actually do, not what they say they want.

Rob Fitzpatrick's “The Mom Test” rule: Talk about their life, not your idea. If your mom says your idea is great, you asked the wrong questions.

When to Walk Away

Sometimes quitting is winning.

#### Walk away if:

Talked to 20+ people, less than five interested

Manual delivery takes 10+ hours per customer with no automation path

Market is less than 10,000 potential customers

They love it but won't pay more than $5/month

You've lost interest in the problem

I spent six weeks validating a podcast editing tool. Talked to 30 editors. 20 said they'd use it. Only two would pay more than $10/month. Market too small. Killed it. Saved six months.

Killing bad ideas fast is smart, not failing.

Is this the best use of my time?

Smart founders validate both sides. The market validates your product. You validate the market.

The Pattern That Separates Winners

#### After studying dozens of validation stories, here's what works:

Successful founders validate ruthlessly before building. Not because they doubt themselves. Because they respect their time too much to waste it on what nobody wants.

Validation isn't about proving you're right. It's about discovering what's true.

Most ideas should die in validation. That's not failure. That's intelligence.

Your job isn't falling in love with your solution. Your job is falling in love with your customer's problem and staying flexible on how to solve it.

Dallas Price from Forum Venture Studio says come up with as many ideas as you can, then kill 85% of them. Get 3–5 committed customers (with contracts) before building your MVP.

Validate first. Build second. Scale third. Always in that order.
